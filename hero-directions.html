The bet

The headline stops selling the tool and asks the visitor a question about themselves. The chips are the campaign's whole “see your ___” list, made clickable — and the answer is a real running notebook, so the proof and the pitch are the same object.

What it fixes

Kills the six auto-scrolling columns. One thing moves at a time, and only when the visitor asks it to. Content drops from ~40 code fragments to one headline, five words, one caption.

Contentful shape

One Hero entry: kicker, headline, two CTAs — plus a repeatable HeroChip (label, notebookSlug, stageTitle, caption rich text). Editors add a chip without touching code. Needs one new content type.

Risk

Above the fold is quiet until someone clicks. If the visitor doesn't engage, they've seen a question and one chart. Mitigation: auto-advance chips once, slowly, then stop on interaction.

What it fixes

Same visual density as today's hero, but every pixel is a result rather than a code fragment, and nothing moves on its own. Grid stays here (it earns its keep) and comes out of the band below, per your note.

The bet

Maximum confidence, minimum content. One headline, one sentence, one chart that animates because the visitor moved a control — which is exactly the thing the current auto-scrolling hero fails to communicate.

What it fixes

The most aggressive answer to “too much content.” Everything that isn't the question or the proof is gone. The breadth-of-use story moves entirely to the bands below.

Contentful shape

Trivially the simplest: headline, subhead, two CTAs, one notebookSlug. Almost certainly buildable against the current model with no new content type.

Risk

One chart has to carry the entire promise. Stacked-to-grouped is a great toy but reads as abstract — no domain, no stakes. May need a version wired to recognizable data (a real budget, a real election) to land the “see your ___” idea.

Where I'd go

A is the campaign. It's the only one of the three where the headline and the interaction are the same idea, and it's the one that scales — every new “see your ___” is a chip, not a redesign. B is the safe ship and the likeliest to survive Contentful without new content types. C is the one to show Mike if the argument is “manufact.com is just what we already have” — it's the opposite of a structure argument, it's a nerve argument. My recommendation: build A, and keep B's tile grid as the band immediately below it, which is also where your commercial/breadth-of-use story lives.
